## what is supervised learning
### Supervised learning In supervised learning, the training set you feed to the algorithm includes the desired solutions, called labels (Figure 1-5).
#### geron1
#####
A typical supervised learning task is classification. The spam filter is a good example of this: it is trained with many example emails along with their class (spam or ham), and it must learn how to classify new emails. Another typical task is to predict a target numeric value, such as the price of a car, given a set of features (mileage, age, brand, etc.). This sort of task is called regression (Figure 1-6).⁠1 To train the system, you need to give it many examples of cars, including both their features and their targets (i.e., their prices).
Note that some regression models can be used for classification as well, and vice versa. For example, logistic regression is commonly used for classification, as it can output a value that corresponds to the probability of belonging to a given class (e.g., 20% chance of being spam).
###### (p. 37). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.



## What is "target" and "label"
###
The words target and label are generally treated as synonyms in supervised learning, but target is more common in regression tasks and label is more common in classification tasks. Moreover, features are sometimes called predictors or attributes. These terms may refer to individual samples (e.g., “this car’s mileage feature is equal to 15,000”) or to all samples (e.g., “the mileage feature is strongly correlated with price”).

## what is unsupervised learning
### Unsupervised learning In unsupervised learning, as you might guess, the training data is unlabeled. The system tries to learn without a teacher.
#### geron1
#####
For example, say you have a lot of data about your blog’s visitors. You may want to run a clustering algorithm to try to detect groups of similar visitors (Figure 1-7). The features may include the user’s age group, their region, their interests, the duration of their sessions, and so on. At no point do you tell the algorithm which group a visitor belongs to: it finds those connections without your help. For example, it might notice that 40% of your visitors are teenagers who love comic books and generally read your blog after school, while 20% are adults who enjoy sci-fi and who visit during the weekends. If you use a hierarchical clustering algorithm, it may also subdivide each group into smaller groups. This may help you target your posts for each group.
###### (p. 39). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.



## What is "dimensionality reduction" and "feature extraction"
###
A related task is dimensionality reduction, in which the goal is to simplify the data without losing too much information. One way to do this is to merge several correlated features into one. For example, a car’s mileage may be strongly correlated with its age, so the dimensionality reduction algorithm will merge them into one feature that represents the car’s wear and tear. This is called feature extraction.
#### geron1
#####
It is often a good idea to try to reduce the number of dimensions in your training data using a dimensionality reduction algorithm before you feed it to another machine learning algorithm (such as a supervised learning algorithm). It will run much faster, the data will take up less disk and memory space, and in some cases it may also perform better.
###### (p. 40). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.



## what is Anomaly detection
###
Yet another important unsupervised task is anomaly detection—for example, detecting unusual credit card transactions to prevent fraud, catching manufacturing defects, or automatically removing outliers from a dataset before feeding it to another learning algorithm. The system is shown mostly normal instances during training, so it learns to recognize them; then, when it sees a new instance, it can tell whether it looks like a normal one or whether it is likely an anomaly (see Figure 1-9).
#### geron1
#####
The features may include distance from home, time of day, day of the week, amount withdrawn, merchant category, transaction frequency, etc. A very similar task is novelty detection: it aims to detect new instances that look different from all instances in the training set. This requires having a very “clean” training set, devoid of any instance that you would like the algorithm to detect. For example, if you have thousands of pictures of dogs, and 1% of these pictures represent Chihuahuas, then a novelty detection algorithm should not treat new pictures of Chihuahuas as novelties. On the other hand, anomaly detection algorithms may consider these dogs as so rare and so different from other dogs that they would likely classify them as anomalies (no offense to Chihuahuas).
###### (p. 41). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.

Finally, another common unsupervised task is association rule learning, in which the goal is to dig into large amounts of data and discover interesting relations between attributes. For example, suppose you own a supermarket. Running an association rule on your sales logs may reveal that people who purchase barbecue sauce and potato chips also tend to buy steak. Thus, you may want to place these items close to one another.

## what is semi-supervised learning
###
Semi-supervised learning Since labeling data is usually time-consuming and costly, you will often have plenty of unlabeled instances, and few labeled instances. Some algorithms can deal with data that’s partially labeled. This is called semi-supervised learning (Figure 1-10).
#### geron1
#####
Some photo-hosting services, such as Google Photos, are good examples of this. Once you upload all your family photos to the service, it automatically recognizes that the same person A shows up in photos 1, 5, and 11, while another person B shows up in photos 2, 5, and 7. This is the unsupervised part of the algorithm (clustering). Now all the system needs is for you to tell it who these people are. Just add one label per person⁠3 and it is able to name everyone in every photo, which is useful for searching photos.
Most semi-supervised learning algorithms are combinations of unsupervised and supervised algorithms. For example, a clustering algorithm may be used to group similar instances together, and then
every unlabeled instance can be labeled with the most common label in its cluster. Once the whole dataset is labeled, it is possible to use any supervised learning algorithm.
###### (p. 43). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.


## what is Self-supervised learning
###
Self-supervised learning Another approach to machine learning involves actually generating a fully labeled dataset from a fully unlabeled one. Again, once the whole dataset is labeled, any supervised learning algorithm can be used. This approach is called self-supervised learning.
#### geron1
#####
For example, if you have a large dataset of unlabeled images, you can randomly mask a small part of each image and then train a model to recover the original image (Figure 1-11). During training, the masked images are used as the inputs to the model, and the original images are used as the labels.
The resulting model may be quite useful in itself—for example, to repair damaged images or to erase unwanted objects from pictures. But more often than not, a model trained using self-supervised learning is not the final goal. You’ll usually want to tweak and fine-tune the model for a slightly different task—one that you actually care about.......

## what is Tansfer learning
###
Transferring knowledge from one task to another is called transfer learning, and it’s one of the most important techniques in machine learning today, especially when using deep neural networks (i.e., neural networks composed of many layers of neurons). We will discuss this in detail in Part II.
#### geron1
#####
As we will see in Chapter 15, large language models (LLMs) are trained in a very similar way, by masking random words in a huge text corpus and training the model to predict the missing words. This large pretrained model can then be fine-tuned for various applications, from sentiment analysis to chatbots. Some people consider self-supervised learning to be a part of unsupervised learning, since it deals with fully unlabeled datasets. But self-supervised learning uses (generated) labels during training, so in that regard it’s closer to supervised learning. And the term “unsupervised learning” is generally used when dealing with tasks like clustering, dimensionality reduction, or anomaly detection, whereas self-supervised learning focuses on the same tasks as supervised learning: mainly classification and regression. In short, it’s best to treat self-supervised learning as its own category.

## Batch learning vs. online learning
###
Another criterion used to classify machine learning systems is whether the system can learn incrementally from a stream of incoming data. For example, random forests (see Chapter 6) can only be trained from scratch on the full dataset—this is called batch learning—while other models can be trained one batch of data at a time, for example, using gradient descent (see Chapter 4)—this is called online learning.
#### geron1
###### (p. 48). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.


## What is Batch learning
###
In batch learning, the system must be trained using all the available data. This will generally take a lot of time and computing resources, so it is typically done offline. First the system is trained, and then it is launched into production and runs without learning anymore; it just applies what it has learned. This is called offline learning.
#### geron1
#####
Unfortunately, a model’s performance tends to decay slowly over time, simply because the world continues to evolve while the model remains unchanged. This phenomenon is often called data drift (or model rot). The solution is to regularly retrain the model on up-to-date data. How often you need to do that depends on the use case: if the model classifies pictures of cats and dogs, its performance will decay very slowly, but if the model deals with fast-evolving systems, for example making predictions on the financial market, then it is likely to decay quite fast.
Even a model trained to classify pictures of cats and dogs may need to be retrained regularly, not because cats and dogs will mutate overnight, but because cameras keep changing, along with image formats, sharpness, brightness, and size ratios. Moreover, people may love different breeds next year, or they may decide to dress their pets with tiny hats—who knows?
######  (p. 48). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition. See page 48 - 50




## What is online learning
### In online learning, you train the system incrementally by feeding it data instances sequentially, either individually or in small groups called mini-batches. Each learning step is fast and cheap, so the system can learn about new data on the fly, as it arrives (see Figure 1-13). The most common online algorithm by far is gradient descent, but there are a few others.
#### geron1
#####
Online learning is useful for systems that need to adapt to change extremely rapidly (e.g., to detect new patterns in the stock market). It is also a good option if you have limited computing resources; for example, if the model is trained on a mobile device. Most importantly, online learning algorithms can be used to train models on huge datasets that cannot fit in one machine’s memory (this is called out-of-core learning). The algorithm loads part of the data, runs a training step on that data, and repeats the process until it has run on all of the data (see Figure 1-14).
###### (p. 50). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.


## what is learning rate
### One important parameter of online learning systems is how fast they should adapt to changing data: this is called the learning rate. If you set a high learning rate, then your system will rapidly adapt to new data, but it will also tend to quickly forget the old data: this is called catastrophic forgetting (or catastrophic interference). You don’t want a spam filter to flag only the latest kinds of spam it was shown! Conversely, if you set a low learning rate, the system will have more inertia; that is, it will learn more slowly, but it will also be less sensitive to noise in the new data or to sequences of nonrepresentative data points (outliers).
#### geron1
#####
Out-of-core learning is usually done offline (i.e., not on the live system), so online learning can be a confusing name. Think of it as incremental learning. Moreover, mini-batches are often just called “batches”, so batch learning is also a confusing name. Think of it as learning from scratch on the full dataset.
###### (pp. 52). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.




## Instance-Based Versus Model-Based Learning
###
Instead of just flagging emails that are identical to known spam emails, your spam filter could be programmed to also flag emails that are very similar to known spam emails. This requires a measure of similarity between two emails. A (very basic) similarity measure between two emails could be to count the number of words they have in common. The system would flag an email as spam if it has many words in common with a known spam email. This is called instance-based learning: the system learns the examples by heart, then generalizes to new cases by using a similarity measure to compare them to the learned examples (or a subset of them). For example, in Figure 1-15 the new instance would be classified as a triangle because the majority of the most similar instances belong to that class. Instance-based learning often shines with small datasets, especially if the data keeps changing, but it does not scale very well: it requires deploying a whole copy of the training set to production; making predictions requires searching for similar instances, which can be quite slow; and it doesn’t work well with high-dimensional data such as images.
#### geron1
#####
Another way to generalize from a set of examples is to build a model of these examples and then use that model to make predictions. This is called model-based learning (Figure 1-16).

## What is a performance measure
###
Before you can use your model, you need to define the parameter values θ0 and θ1. How can you know which values will make your model perform best? To answer this question, you need to specify a performance measure. You can either define a utility function (or fitness function) that measures how good your model is, or you can define a cost function (a.k.a., loss function) that measures how bad it is. For linear regression problems, people typically use a cost function that measures the distance between the linear model’s predictions and the training examples; the objective is to minimize this distance.
This is where the linear regression algorithm comes in: you feed it your training examples, and it finds the parameters that make the linear model fit best to your data. This is called training the model. In our case, the algorithm finds that the optimal parameter values are θ0 = 3.75 and θ1 = 6.78 × 10–5.
Now the model fits the training data as closely as possible (for a linear model), as you can see in Figure 1-19.
You are finally ready to run the model to make predictions. For example, say you want to know how happy Puerto Ricans are, and the OECD data does not have the answer. Warning
Overfitting happens when the model is too complex relative to the amount and noisiness of the training data, so it starts to learn random patterns in the training data. Here are possible solutions: Simplify the model by selecting one with fewer parameters (e.g., a linear model rather than a high-degree polynomial model), by reducing the number of attributes in the training data, or by constraining the model. ## what is a generalization error
###
split your data into two sets: the training set and the test set. As these names imply, you train your model using the training set, and you test it using the test set. The error rate on new cases is called the generalization error (or out-of-sample error), and by evaluating your model on the test set, you get an estimate of this error. This value tells you how well your model will perform on instances it has never seen before. ## why do you use a validation set?
###
Now suppose that the linear model generalizes better, but you want to apply some regularization to avoid overfitting. The question is, how do you choose the value of the regularization hyperparameter? One option is to train 100 different models using 100 different values for this hyperparameter. Suppose you find the best hyperparameter value that produces a model with the lowest generalization error⁠—say, just 5% error. You launch this model into production, but unfortunately it does not perform as well as expected and produces 15% errors. What just happened? The problem is that you measured the generalization error multiple times on the test set, and you adapted the model and hyperparameters to produce the best model for that particular set. This means the model is unlikely to perform as well on new data.
A common solution to this problem is called holdout validation (Figure 1-25): you simply hold out part of the training set to evaluate several candidate models and select the best one. The new held-out set is called the validation set
#### geron1
#####
A common solution to this problem is called holdout validation (Figure 1-25): you simply hold out part of the training set to evaluate several candidate models and select the best one. The new held-out set is called the validation set (or the development set, or dev set). More specifically, you train multiple models with various hyperparameters on the reduced training set (i.e., the full training set minus the validation set), and you select the model that performs best on the validation set. After this holdout validation process, you train the best model on the full training set (including the validation set), and this gives you the final model. Lastly, you evaluate this final model on the test set to get an estimate of the generalization error.
###### (pp. 77-78). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.
cross-validation:
This solution usually works quite well. However, if the validation set is too small, then the model evaluations will be imprecise: you may end up selecting a suboptimal model by mistake. Conversely, if the validation set is too large, then the remaining training set will be much smaller than the full training set. Why is this bad? Well, since the final model will be trained on the full training set, it is not ideal to compare candidate models trained on a much smaller training set. It would be like selecting the fastest sprinter to participate in a marathon. One way to solve this problem is to perform repeated "cross-validation", using many small validation sets. ---Each model is evaluated once per validation set--- after it is trained on the rest of the data. By averaging out all the evaluations of a model, you get a much more accurate measure of its performance. There is a drawback, however: the training time is multiplied by the number of validation sets.

## What is a labeled training set?
###
A labeled training set is a collection of data examples where each input (example) has been paired with the correct output (also called the label, target, or ground truth).

## Would you frame the problem of spam detection as a supervised learning problem or an unsupervised learning problem?
###
Spam detection is a classic classification task:
You have emails (the input).
You want to predict a discrete label: “spam” or “not spam” (sometimes “ham”)
In supervised learning, the model learns the mapping from input → label using a labeled training set.
For every email in the training data, humans (or reliable rules) have already provided the correct answer: “this is spam” or “this is not spam”.
The algorithm then learns patterns that distinguish spam from legitimate emails (e.g., certain words, sender patterns, links, formatting, etc.) so it can predict the label for new, unseen emails.
Why not unsupervised?
Unsupervised learning has no labels. It would try to discover natural groups in the emails (e.g., clustering similar emails together). While you might end up with one cluster that looks like spam and another that looks like normal mail, you would still need to manually inspect the clusters and assign the “spam” label afterward. This is less accurate, harder to evaluate, and much slower to improve compared to supervised learning.

## What is out-of-core learning?

## What type of algorithm relies on a similarity measure to make predictions?
###
Instance-based learning (also called memory-based learning or lazy learning) is the type of algorithm that relies on a similarity measure to make predictions.
Why "Instance-Based"?
Instead of learning a fixed model (like a neural network or decision tree) during training, these algorithms store the training data (the instances) in memory.
When a new prediction is needed, they compare the new example to all (or many) stored instances using a similarity measure (or distance metric) and base the prediction directly on the most similar ones.
They are called lazy because they do almost no work during the "training" phase — they just store the data. All the computation happens at prediction time.

## Can you name four of the main challenges in machine learning?
###
1: Overfitting vs. Underfitting (The Generalization Problem)
Description: The model performs very well on the training data but poorly on new, unseen data (overfitting), or it performs poorly even on the training data (underfitting).
Why it's hard: Models can easily memorize noise or irrelevant patterns instead of learning the true underlying relationship.
Common causes: Too complex a model (too many parameters), too little data, or noisy labels.
How we fight it: Regularization, cross-validation, more data, simpler models, or early stopping.
2: Data Quality and Quantity
Description: Machine learning is extremely data-hungry, but real-world data is often insufficient, dirty, biased, incomplete, or imbalanced.
Key issues:
Not enough labeled data (especially for supervised learning)
Class imbalance (e.g., 99% non-spam vs. 1% spam)
Noisy or incorrect labels
Biased data that leads to unfair models
Impact: Even the best algorithms fail if the data is poor.
3: Concept Drift / Non-Stationary Data
Description: The statistical properties of the target variable or input features change over time, so the model that worked yesterday stops working today.
Examples:
Spam detection: spammers constantly evolve their tactics
Customer behavior: preferences shift due to trends or economic changes
Robot walking: terrain or wear-and-tear changes
Why difficult: Most traditional training assumes the data distribution stays the same (i.i.d. assumption).
4: Scalability and Computational Cost
Description: Training and deploying modern models (especially deep learning) requires enormous amounts of data, compute power, memory, and energy.
Challenges include:
Out-of-core learning for massive datasets
Long training times (weeks on clusters of GPUs)
High inference cost in production
Difficulty running models on edge devices (phones, robots)
Related issues: Hyperparameter tuning at scale and efficient distributed training.

## If your model performs great on the training data but generalizes poorly to new instances, what is happening? Can you name three possible solutions?
###
This is a classic case of overfitting.
The model has memorized the training data (including noise, quirks, and irrelevant patterns) instead of learning the true underlying patterns. As a result:
It achieves very high accuracy / low error on the training set.
It performs poorly on new, unseen data (validation or test set).
In other words, the model has low bias but very high variance — it fits the training data too closely and fails to generalize.
#### geron1 chapterquestions
#####
1: Get More Training Data (often the most effective)
More diverse and representative data helps the model learn the real signal instead of noise.
Techniques: Collect additional labeled examples, use data augmentation (especially useful in computer vision and NLP), or generate synthetic data.
Why it works: With enough data, even complex models are forced to generalize rather than memorize.
2: Regularization
Add a penalty to the loss function that discourages overly complex models.
Common types:
L1 / L2 regularization (adds penalty on large weights)
Dropout (randomly turns off neurons during training in neural networks)
Early stopping (stop training when validation performance stops improving)
Why it works: It constrains the model’s capacity so it cannot fit every detail in the training set.
3: Simplify the Model (Reduce Model Complexity)
Use a less powerful model with fewer parameters or lower capacity.
Examples:
Reduce the depth or number of neurons in a neural network
Use a shallower decision tree or prune it
Switch from a complex ensemble to a simpler linear model
Reduce the value of k in k-NN or increase regularization strength
Why it works: A simpler model has lower variance and is less likely to overfit.

## What is a test set, and why would you want to use it?
###
A test set is a portion of your dataset that is kept completely separate from the training process and is only used after the model is fully trained to evaluate its final performance.
Training set: Data the model sees and learns from (used to adjust parameters).
Validation set (optional): Data used during development to tune hyperparameters and monitor overfitting.
Test set: The final, untouched “exam” data that the model has never seen before.
The test set should be treated like real-world new data that arrives after deployment.
#### geron1 chapterquestions
#####
Here’s why the test set is essential:
1:Unbiased Estimate of Real-World Performance
The test set gives you the most honest measure of how well your model will perform on new, unseen instances.
Training accuracy can be misleading (especially if the model is overfitting).
2: Detect Overfitting
If your model does great on the training data but poorly on the test set → clear sign of overfitting (exactly the problem we discussed earlier).
3: Compare Models Fairly
You can reliably compare different algorithms, architectures, or hyperparameter settings using the test set as the final judge.
4: Avoid Data Leakage
By never using the test set during training or hyperparameter tuning, you prevent the model from indirectly “seeing” the test data, which would give overly optimistic results.

## What is the purpose of a validation set?
###
The purpose of a validation set is to provide an unbiased estimate of model performance during development, so you can make good decisions about the model without touching the test set.
While the test set is reserved strictly for the final evaluation (like a final exam), the validation set is used during the training and tuning phase as a temporary checkpoint.
Its main purposes are:
1: Monitor for Overfitting
You compare performance on the training set vs. the validation set after each epoch (or iteration).
2: Hyperparameter Tuning
You try different values for hyperparameters (learning rate, number of layers, tree depth, value of k in k-NN, regularization strength, etc.) and choose the combination that performs best on the validation set.
3: Model Selection
When comparing different algorithms or architectures (e.g., neural network vs. random forest vs. logistic regression), the validation set helps you pick the best one before the final test.
4: Guide Training Decisions
Decide whether to add more data, apply stronger regularization, simplify the model, or change the architecture.
Detect issues like underfitting early.
#### geron1 chapterquestions
###### (p. 82). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.
Typical split: 70–80% training, 10–15% validation, 10–15% test.
The validation set should be representative of the real data (same distribution as test set).
In cross-validation (especially with small datasets), you rotate the validation portions so every part of the data gets used for validation at some point.
Once you finish tuning and pick your final model, you train it on the combined training + validation data, then evaluate only once on the test set.


## What is the train-dev set, when do you need it, and how do you use it?
###
The train-dev set (also called training-development set) is a separate subset of data taken from the same distribution as the training set. It is used specifically to diagnose overfitting (high variance) when the training set and the regular development/validation set (dev set) come from different distributions.
In modern machine learning (especially deep learning), data often comes from different sources:
Training data might come from one source (e.g., older data, synthetic data, or data collected in one region).
The dev set (used for hyperparameter tuning and model selection) might come from a slightly different distribution (e.g., newer data, real user data, or data from another country).
When distributions differ, a large gap between training error and dev error could be caused by either:
Overfitting to the training data, or
Distribution shift (data mismatch) between training and dev.
The train-dev set helps you separate these two problems.
#### geron1 chapterquestions
#####
When Do You Need a Train-Dev Set?
You need it mainly in these situations:
Your training data and dev/test data come from different distributions (common in real-world projects).
You want to clearly diagnose whether poor dev-set performance is due to overfitting (model memorizes training data) or data mismatch (the model hasn't seen this kind of data before).
You are working on large-scale or production systems where distribution shift is likely (e.g., spam detection, recommendation systems, autonomous driving, speech recognition).
If your training, dev, and test sets all come from the exact same distribution, you usually don’t need a separate train-dev set — a regular validation/dev set is enough.

## What can go wrong if you tune hyperparameters using the test set?
###
It leads to overly optimistic results and a model that performs much worse in the real world than you expect.
What Goes Wrong: "Test Set Overfitting" (or "Information Leakage")
When you use the test set to tune hyperparameters (learning rate, number of layers, tree depth, value of k in k-NN, regularization strength, etc.), the following happens:
The model indirectly "sees" the test set during development.
Your hyperparameter choices become optimized specifically for the test set, not for truly new data.
The test set stops being an unbiased measure of generalization — it turns into just another validation set.
As a result, the final performance numbers you report (accuracy, F1-score, etc.) are inflated and misleading.
#### geron1 chapterquestions
#####
Real-World Consequences
You deploy the model thinking it achieves 95% accuracy, but in production it drops to 82%.
You believe your model is state-of-the-art, but it’s actually average or below average.
Scientific papers or project reports become irreproducible or dishonest.
You waste time and resources because your diagnosis of the model’s strengths and weaknesses is wrong.
This problem is sometimes called “cheating on the final exam” — you peeked at the answers while studying, so your score no longer reflects real knowledge.

4: recall: recall = (True Positives) / (True positives + False negatives).
(“Out of all the things that were positive, how many did my model catch?”)
Now consider a model that only makes positive predictions when it’s extremely confident. Obviously, such a classifier would not be very useful, since it would ignore all but one positive instance. It is often convenient to combine precision and recall into a single metric called the F1 score, especially when you need a single metric to compare two classifiers. The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall (Equation 3-3). Whereas the regular mean treats all values equally, the harmonic mean gives much more weight to low values. As a result, the classifier will only get a high F1 score if both recall and precision are high.

## How does a linear (regression) model make predictions?
###
More generally, a linear model makes a prediction by simply computing a weighted sum of the input features, plus a constant called the bias term (also called the intercept term),
......(from AI:)
A linear model predicts the output (like house price or salary) by doing something very straightforward:
Prediction = (w₁ × feature₁) + (w₂ × feature₂) + ... + (wₙ × featureₙ) + b.
Each w (weight) tells how important that input feature is.
A big positive weight means “when this feature increases, the prediction goes up a lot”.
The b (bias term or intercept) is a constant that shifts the whole line up or down even when all features are zero.
That’s it — just a weighted sum of the inputs plus a bias.

Cons:
Requires explicitly inverting a matrix → numerically unstable if features are correlated (multicollinear) or the matrix is ill-conditioned. Small rounding errors can blow up.
Computationally expensive or impossible for large data: O(n³) or worse when there are thousands of features (or millions of examples). Memory-heavy too.
Fails completely if XᵀX is singular (not invertible).
Best for: Small/medium, clean datasets where you want a quick exact answer (e.g., < 1000 features and not too many rows).
2. SVD (Singular Value Decomposition) — the stable way to solve least squares
How it works:
Decomposes the feature matrix as X = U Σ Vᵀ.
Then uses the pseudoinverse: w = V Σ⁺ Uᵀ y
(Σ⁺ inverts only the meaningful singular values and sets tiny ones near zero to exactly zero.)
Pros:
Extremely numerically stable — avoids the instability of forming and inverting XᵀX.
Gracefully handles multicollinear/redundant features, ill-conditioned matrices, or rank-deficient cases (some features add no new information).
Still gives an essentially exact solution (up to floating-point precision).
Many real libraries (like NumPy's lstsq) use SVD (or closely related methods like QR) under the hood for this reason.
Cons:
A bit slower than a perfect normal equation on tiny, well-behaved data.
Still struggles with truly massive datasets (millions of rows + thousands of features) due to memory and computation cost of the decomposition.
Best for: Most practical “medium-sized” datasets in statistics/ML, especially when data might have correlated features. Safer and more reliable than raw normal equation.
3. Gradient Descent (and variants like stochastic gradient descent (SGD), mini-batch GD)
How it works:
Start with random weights → repeatedly take small steps in the direction that reduces the error (following the gradient/slope of the cost function). Iterative process.
Pros:
Highly scalable to huge datasets (millions or billions of examples) and high-dimensional data.
Low memory use (especially stochastic/mini-batch versions — you don’t need to load the entire matrix at once).
Works even when normal equation or SVD would be too slow or run out of memory.
Easily extended to regularization, non-linear models, and deep learning.
Cons:
Not exact — it approximates the solution and may need many iterations.
Requires tuning (learning rate, number of epochs, batch size).
Can get stuck in local minima or converge slowly if hyperparameters are bad (though linear regression is convex, so it’s usually fine).
Best for: Large-scale real-world problems, online learning, or when you’re already in a deep learning framework.

## Gradient descent and cost function
###
AI:
Core Idea of Gradient Descent
Gradient Descent is a general-purpose optimization algorithm used to minimize a cost function (also called loss function or error function).
It works by iteratively tweaking the model's parameters (usually denoted by the vector θ — theta).
The goal is to find the values of θ that make the cost function as small as possible.
How it works (the intuition):
Imagine you're lost in foggy mountains and can only feel the slope under your feet.
Gradient descent does the same: at every step, it measures the local gradient (slope) of the cost function with respect to each parameter in θ.
It then moves in the direction of the steepest descent (downhill) to reduce the cost.
You start with random initialization (θ is filled with random values), then repeatedly take small steps until the algorithm converges (reaches a point where the gradient is zero or nearly zero, meaning you've found a minimum).
Key Hyperparameter: Learning Rate
The learning rate (often denoted η — eta) controls the size of the steps the algorithm takes.
Too small learning rate: The algorithm converges very slowly (takes many iterations).
Too large learning rate: The algorithm can overshoot the minimum, bounce around, or even diverge (get worse and worse).
Choosing a good learning rate is crucial.
Challenges with Gradient Descent
Real cost functions are often not perfect bowls. They can have:
Local minima — points that look like a minimum locally but are not the best overall.
Plateaus (flat regions) — where the gradient is very small, so progress is extremely slow.
Ridges, holes, and irregular terrain — making convergence difficult.
Because of these, where you start (random initialization) matters a lot. You might get stuck in a local minimum instead of reaching the global minimum (the absolute best solution).
Convex Cost Functions (Very Important Concept)
A cost function is convex if:
Any line segment drawn between two points on the curve never goes below the curve.
In simple terms: the function has a bowl-like shape with no local minima, only one global minimum.
#### geron4
#####
When the cost function is convex (and continuous, with no sudden jumps in slope):
Gradient descent is guaranteed to eventually reach the global minimum (as long as the learning rate isn’t too large and you run it long enough).
Example mentioned: The Mean Squared Error (MSE) cost function used in Linear Regression is convex. This is why gradient descent works very reliably for linear regression.
Effect of Feature Scaling
Even with a convex cost function, the shape of the "bowl" can be problematic:
If features have very different scales (e.g., one feature ranges from 0–1, another from 0–1000), the cost function becomes a long, narrow, elongated bowl.
In this case, gradient descent can take a zigzag path and converge much more slowly.
Solution (implied): It is highly recommended to scale the features (e.g., using standardization or normalization) so they all have similar ranges. This makes the cost function more symmetric and helps gradient descent converge faster.
Summary of Main Terminology.
Cost Function / Loss Function: The function we want to minimize (measures how bad the model’s predictions are).
Parameters (θ): The values the algorithm learns (weights and bias in linear regression, for example).
Gradient: The vector of partial derivatives of the cost function w.r.t. each parameter. It tells you the direction and steepness of the slope.
Learning Rate (η): Controls step size.
Convergence: When the algorithm stops improving significantly (reaches a minimum).
Global Minimum: The absolute lowest point of the cost function.
Local Minimum: A minimum that is not the global one.
Convex Function: A cost function with only one global minimum (no local minima).
Random Initialization: Starting with random parameter values.

## What is Stochastic gradient descent
###
Stochastic Gradient Descent The main problem with batch gradient descent is the fact that it uses the whole training set to compute the gradients at every step, which makes it very slow when the training set is large. At the opposite extreme, stochastic gradient descent picks a random instance in the training set at every step and computes the gradients based only on that single instance. Obviously, working on a single instance at a time makes the algorithm much faster because it has very little data to manipulate at every iteration. It also makes it possible to train on huge training sets, since only one instance needs to be in memory at each iteration (stochastic GD can be implemented as an out-of-core algorithm; see Chapter 1). On the other hand, due to its stochastic (i.e., random) nature, this algorithm is much less regular than batch gradient descent: instead of gently decreasing until it reaches the minimum, the cost function will bounce up and down, decreasing only on average. Over time it will end up very close to the minimum, but once it gets there it will continue to bounce around, never settling down (see Figure 4-9). Once the algorithm stops, the final parameter values will be good, but not optimal.
#### geron4
#####
Therefore, randomness is good to escape from local optima, but bad because it means that the algorithm can never settle at the minimum. One solution to this dilemma is to gradually reduce the learning rate. The steps start out large (which helps make quick progress and escape local minima), then get smaller and smaller, allowing the algorithm to settle at the global minimum. This process is akin to simulated annealing, an algorithm inspired by the process in metallurgy of annealing, where molten metal is slowly cooled down. The function that determines the learning rate at each iteration is called the learning schedule. If the learning rate is reduced too quickly, you may get stuck in a local minimum, or even end up frozen halfway to the minimum. If the learning rate is reduced too slowly, you may jump around the minimum for a long time and end up with a suboptimal solution if you halt training too early.

## The Bias/Variance trade-off
###
An important theoretical result of statistics and machine learning is the fact that a model’s generalization error can be expressed as the sum of three very different errors:
1: Bias:  This part of the generalization error is due to wrong assumptions, such as assuming that the data is linear when it is actually quadratic. A high-bias model is most likely to underfit the training data.⁠6
2: Variance:  This part is due to the model’s excessive sensitivity to small variations in the training data. A model with many degrees of freedom (such as a high-degree polynomial model) is likely to have high variance and thus overfit the training data.
3: Irreducible error:  This part is due to the noisiness of the data itself. The only way to reduce this part of the error is to clean up the data (e.g., fix the data sources, such as broken sensors, or detect and remove outliers). Increasing a model’s complexity will typically increase its variance and reduce its bias. Conversely, reducing a model’s complexity (or increasing regularization) increases its bias and reduces its variance (see Figure 4-17). This is why it is called a trade-off.

## Which linear regression training algorithm can you use if you have a training set with millions of features?
###
AI:
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) or Mini-batch Gradient Descent (and Batch Gradient Descent if the full training set fits in memory).
These gradient-descent variants scale well with a very large number of features n (millions), because each step’s computational cost is linear in n (and does not require loading the entire dataset into memory for SGD or mini-batch). ## Suppose the features in your training set have very different scales. Which algorithms might suffer from this, and how? What can you do about it?
###
AI:
Gradient Descent (including its variants like Batch GD, Stochastic GD, and Mini-batch GD) is the main algorithm that suffers significantly when features have very different scales.
...
Why Gradient Descent Suffers:
The cost function (e.g., Mean Squared Error in linear regression) becomes elongated or "bowl-shaped" with very narrow valleys in one direction and wide in others.
Gradient updates become imbalanced: features with larger scales produce much larger partial derivatives, so the algorithm takes zig-zagging or overly large steps along those axes.
Result: much slower convergence (it can take far more iterations to reach the minimum), potential instability, or difficulty finding the global optimum efficiently. In extreme cases, a poor learning rate choice can cause divergence.
This issue appears in any model trained with gradient descent, such as:
Linear/Logistic Regression (when using GD instead of the Normal Equation),
Neural networks,
Support Vector Machines (when using gradient-based solvers)
#### geron4 chapterquestions
#####
Algorithms That Generally Do NOT Suffer (or Suffer Much Less)
1:
Normal Equation or SVD for linear regression: These are closed-form solutions and are invariant to feature scaling (though scaling can still help numerically in practice).
2:Tree-based models (Decision Trees, Random Forests, Gradient Boosting): They make splits based on thresholds and are scale-invariant.
3:
Regularized linear models (Ridge, Lasso) when solved with the closed-form method: They can still benefit from scaling for numerical stability, especially with regularization penalties.
----
What Can You Do About It? (Feature Scaling)
Scale (or standardize) the features before training so they all have roughly the same range/scale. Two common techniques from Géron's book:
Min-max scaling (Normalization)
Rescales values to [0, 1]:
$  x' = \frac{x - \min(x)}{\max(x) - \min(x)}  $
Use sklearn.preprocessing.MinMaxScaler. Good when you know bounds or want bounded features.
Standardization (Z-score normalization) — often preferred for gradient descent
Centers features around 0 with unit variance:
$  x' = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}  $ (mean μ = 0, std σ = 1 after scaling)
Use sklearn.preprocessing.StandardScaler. Less sensitive to outliers than min-max.
Recommendation: Use standardization for most gradient-descent-based models (including linear regression with SGD). Apply it consistently to training, validation, and test sets (fit the scaler only on the training set).

Practical Tip (from the book)
When using Gradient Descent, ensure all features have a similar scale—or it will take much longer to converge. Feature scaling is usually done in a preprocessing pipeline (e.g., with sklearn.pipeline.Pipeline).
In short: Gradient Descent slows down dramatically with unscaled features due to distorted cost contours. Fix it by standardizing or normalizing the input features. Tree-based or closed-form methods are largely unaffected.


## Can gradient descent get stuck in a local minimum when training a logistic regression model?
###
No, gradient descent cannot get stuck in a local minimum when training a logistic regression model.
Why Not?
The cost function for logistic regression (also called the log-loss or cross-entropy loss) is convex. A convex function has only one global minimum—no local minima exist (except the global one itself).
---
Gradient descent (Batch, Stochastic, or Mini-batch) is therefore guaranteed to converge to the global minimum, provided:
The learning rate is not too large (otherwise it may diverge or oscillate).
You run it long enough.
---
This property holds because logistic regression is a linear model (the decision boundary is linear in the feature space). The combination of the linear predictor with the logistic (sigmoid) function produces a convex optimization problem.

In non-convex problems (e.g., training deep neural networks), the cost surface has many local minima, saddle points, and plateaus. Gradient descent can get stuck in poor local minima there.
For linear regression (with MSE loss), the cost function is also convex, so the same guarantee applies.

## Do all gradient descent algorithms lead to the same model, provided you let them run long enough?
###
AI: No, not all gradient descent algorithms will lead to exactly the same model, even if you let them run for a very long time—though they will usually produce very similar models when the problem is convex (as is the case for linear and logistic regression).
Detailed Explanation (from Géron’s book, Chapter 4)
Batch Gradient Descent (BGD):
With a fixed (well-chosen) learning rate and a convex cost function, it will eventually converge to the exact global optimum. If you run it long enough, it stops at essentially the same parameter values every time (assuming the same initialization and no numerical issues).
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) and Mini-batch Gradient Descent:
These algorithms are stochastic (they use randomness—random instance or random batch selection at each step).
Even on a convex problem, they never truly converge to a single point if the learning rate stays constant. Instead, they keep bouncing around the global optimum.
After a very long time, the parameters will fluctuate in a small region around the optimal solution. Different random seeds or different runs will therefore produce slightly different final models.

#####
How to Make SGD / Mini-batch GD Reach a Similar Final Model
To get closer to true convergence (and more consistent results across runs):
Gradually reduce the learning rate over time (learning rate scheduling, e.g., learning_rate = learning_rate0 / (1 + t) or using schedulers in scikit-learn).
With a properly decaying learning rate, both SGD and mini-batch GD can get arbitrarily close to the global optimum, just like Batch GD.

## Suppose you use batch gradient descent and you plot the validation error at every epoch. If you notice that the validation error consistently goes up, what is likely going on? How can you fix this?
###
The model is likely diverging due to a learning rate that is too high.
What Is Happening?
---In Batch Gradient Descent, the entire training set is used to compute the gradient at each step (epoch). If the learning rate (η) is too large, the algorithm takes overly big steps in parameter space. Instead of descending toward the minimum of the cost function, it can overshoot it repeatedly — or even move farther away — causing the training error (and validation error) to increase at every epoch.
This is a classic sign of divergence rather than convergence.

Plot both training error and validation error per epoch:
If both are going up → almost certainly the learning rate is too high (the updates are destabilizing the optimization).
If training error is decreasing (or stable) while validation error is increasing → this points to overfitting instead (the model is memorizing the training data but failing to generalize).

---Lower the learning rate (the most common and direct fix).

Add feature scaling (standardization with StandardScaler is usually best for GD) — unscaled features with very different ranges make a good learning rate hard to choose.

---If the training error is not increasing (i.e., overfitting case):
Add regularization (e.g., Ridge or Lasso for linear models).

## Is it a good idea to stop mini-batch gradient descent immediately when the validation error goes up?
###
No, it is not a good idea to stop mini-batch gradient descent immediately when the validation error goes up.
Why Not?
Mini-batch Gradient Descent (and Stochastic GD) is noisy by nature. Each update is computed on a small random subset of the training data, so the validation error curve is erratic—it fluctuates up and down even while the model is overall making progress toward the optimum. A single increase in validation error may simply be due to a “bad” mini-batch, not because the model has started overfitting or reached the best possible point.
If you stop at the first upward tick, you risk halting training too early, before the algorithm has had a chance to find a better solution nearby.
---
Recommended Approach (Early Stopping with Patience)
Instead of stopping immediately, use a more robust early stopping strategy:
---Continue training even after the validation error increases.
Keep track of the best validation error seen so far (and save the corresponding model parameters).
Stop only if the validation error has not improved for a certain number of epochs (or mini-batch steps) — this “patience” value is a hyperparameter (e.g., 10 or 50 epochs).
At the end, revert to the model parameters that achieved the lowest validation error.
---This way you tolerate the natural jitter of mini-batch GD while still preventing overfitting and excessive training time.

#####
Comparison with Batch Gradient Descent
---With Batch GD, the validation error is much smoother. If it consistently goes up, it usually signals divergence (learning rate too high) or the start of overfitting. Stopping or lowering the learning rate is more straightforward.
Mini-batch GD’s randomness makes immediate stopping unreliable.

---
High bias + small gap → reduce α (less regularization).
(For reference: if you had a large gap with low training error, you would increase α to fight overfitting.)
---
This matches the diagnosis Géron explains with the learning curves in the regularization section of Chapter 4.
###### (p. 303). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.



##
Why would you want to use:
A: Ridge regression instead of plain linear regression (i.e., without any regularization)?
B: Lasso instead of ridge regression?
C: Elastic net instead of lasso regression?
###
A: Why use Ridge regression instead of plain linear regression (no regularization)?
You would use Ridge when:
Your model suffers from overfitting (high variance), especially when there are many features or when features are highly correlated (multicollinearity).
You want to constrain the model’s weights to prevent them from becoming too large, which improves generalization.
Plain linear regression (ordinary least squares) has no regularization, so it can produce very large coefficients that make the model sensitive to small changes in the training data.
Ridge adds an L2 penalty (α × Σ w_i²) to the cost function. This forces the weights to be smaller (but never exactly zero), which reduces variance with only a small increase in bias.
Result: better performance on unseen data in most practical cases, especially when the number of features is large or when features are correlated.
#### geron4 chapterquestions
#####
B: Why use Lasso instead of Ridge regression?
You would prefer Lasso when:
You want automatic feature selection — Lasso can completely eliminate (set to exactly zero) the weights of the least useful features.
You suspect that only a small subset of your features are actually important (sparse model).
---
Lasso uses an L1 penalty (α × Σ |w_i|). Because of the absolute value, the optimization process tends to drive some coefficients all the way to zero.
Advantages over Ridge:
---
Produces sparser models (fewer non-zero weights).
Easier to interpret (only the selected features remain).
Works well when you have many irrelevant features.
---
Limitation of Lasso: It can behave badly when features are strongly correlated — it tends to pick one feature arbitrarily and set the others to zero.
###### (p. 303). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.
C: Why use Elastic Net instead of Lasso regression?
You should generally prefer Elastic Net over pure Lasso when:
---
Your dataset has groups of strongly correlated features (Lasso’s main weakness).
You want the benefits of both L1 and L2 regularization: sparsity + stability.
---
Elastic Net mixes both penalties:
---
L1 term (like Lasso) → encourages sparsity (some coefficients go to zero).
L2 term (like Ridge) → encourages grouping of correlated features and stabilizes the selection.
---
Rule of thumb from Géron:
Always prefer Elastic Net over pure Lasso.
Plain Ridge is usually a good default.
Use Lasso or Elastic Net only when you expect (or want) many features to have exactly zero coefficients.
---
Summary (quick reference):
Plain Linear → baseline, no regularization.
Ridge → good default when you need regularization but want all features kept.
Lasso → when you want automatic feature selection (but beware of correlated features).
Elastic Net → best of both worlds; usually preferred over Lasso.
These distinctions are exactly what Géron explains in the regularization section of Chapter 4.



## Suppose you want to classify pictures as outdoor/indoor and daytime/nighttime. Should you implement two logistic regression classifiers or one softmax regression classifier?
###
You should implement two separate logistic regression classifiers.
Why two logistic regressions (not one softmax)?
---
The two classification tasks are independent:
Outdoor vs Indoor (binary)
Daytime vs Nighttime (binary)
---
A picture can belong to any combination of the two attributes:
Outdoor + Daytime
Outdoor + Nighttime
Indoor + Daytime
Indoor + Nighttime
---
This is a multi-output classification problem (also called multi-label in a broad sense here), not a single mutually exclusive multi-class problem.
#### geron4 chapterquestions
#####
Softmax regression (multinomial logistic regression) is designed for problems where there is exactly one correct class out of several mutually exclusive classes. It forces the probabilities to sum to 1 across all classes. If you tried to combine both tasks into one softmax with 4 classes (Outdoor-Day, Outdoor-Night, Indoor-Day, Indoor-Night), you would be forcing the model to choose only one combination, which is incorrect — and you would lose the ability to predict, for example, “this indoor scene could be either day or night” with proper independent probabilities.
###### (p. 303). O'Reilly Media. Kindle Edition.
Recommended approach (as explained by Géron):
Train two independent binary logistic regression classifiers:
---
One classifier for Outdoor vs Indoor
One classifier for Daytime vs Nighttime
---
Each classifier outputs its own probability (using the logistic/sigmoid function).
You can then combine the predictions freely:
---
Probability(outdoor) + Probability(indoor) = 1
Probability(daytime) + Probability(nighttime) = 1
---
This gives you all four combinations with independent probabilities, which is exactly what you need.
When would you use softmax instead?
Only if the tasks were mutually exclusive and you had to choose exactly one class (e.g., classifying an image into one of: “outdoor daytime”, “outdoor nighttime”, “indoor daytime”, “indoor nighttime” as a single 4-class problem with no overlap allowed). That is not the case here.
Bottom line:
For this problem → two separate logistic regression classifiers is the correct and simpler solution. Softmax would be inappropriate because the classes are not mutually exclusive.
(This matches Géron’s discussion on logistic regression vs softmax regression in the classification chapter.)

## Centralitets mål - for de fire datatyper
###
--- nominel data: vi kan kun bruge “Mode”, fordi der ikke er en raekkefoelge
--- ordinel data:   her kan vi bruge Mode eller Median == fordi vi ikke kan dele I to.
--- Interval data:  (man bruge Mode, men ikke rigtigt)   vi kan godt bruge median.   (man kan ikke rigtigt bruge “Mean”).
--- ratio data:      (man bruge Mode, men ikke rigtigt) vi kan godt bruge median. Herudover kan vi ogsaa bruge “Mean”.
--- Hvorfor kan man ikke rigtigt snakke om “Mode”?
Problemet er om det data man har er “continous” eller diskret.
#### chrnoter
#####
AI:
Nominal (Categories only= no order): Mode only ==
You can only identify the most frequent category. Mean and median have no meaning.
(Nominal data has no order and is always discrete, so the only appropriate measure of centrality is the mode)
---
Ordinal (Ordered categories =ranks): Median (preferred) and  == You can talk about "middle" position, but not equal distances, so mean is usually inappropriate.
(Ordinal data has a natural order, which allows us to use the median as the preferred measure, and because it is always discrete we can also use the mode.)
---
Interval (Numeric with equal intervals, no true zero): Mean, Median, [and Mode***] == Differences are meaningful (e.g., temperature in °C), so the arithmetic mean works.
(Interval data has equal intervals between values but no true zero, so we can use both the mean and the median; the mode is only practical when the data is discrete or binned.)
---
Ratio (Numeric with equal intervals + true zero):
Mean, Median [and Mode***].
(Ratio data has equal intervals and a true absolute zero, so we can freely use the mean and the median, while the mode is only useful when the data is discrete.)
######
